## Recurring Revenue (HVAC-Specific Thresholds)

| Metric | Green | Caution | Red Flag |
|--------|-------|---------|----------|
| Active maintenance agreements | >200 | 50–200 | <50 |
| Residential/commercial split | <60% residential | 60–80% residential | >80% residential (volatility risk) |
| Maintenance agreement attach rate | >40% of install customers | 20–40% | <20% |

Notes:
- Residential maintenance agreements are valuable but seasonal and more prone to churn than commercial
- Commercial maintenance = longer contracts, higher per-unit value, compliance dimension
- Ask to see the actual maintenance agreement roster — count alone is insufficient; verify active status and last service date

## Technician Certification (HVAC-Specific)

| Certification | Requirement |
|---------------|------------|
| EPA 608 | Required for all refrigerant work — verify every technician |
| State HVAC contractor license | Verify license is in business name (not owner-only) |
| NATE certification | Preferred for senior techs — signals training investment |

Regulatory flags:
- EPA refrigerant violations → automatic deeper review
- License in owner's name only → deal-killer (per thesis)
- Unpermitted installations in prior work → liability risk

## Seasonal Cash Flow Assessment

HVAC revenue is reasonably consistent (maintenance contracts) but demand peaks in summer/winter. Shoulder seasons (spring/fall) are revenue troughs.

Screen for:
- Does the operator model cash on a 13-week rolling basis? (Green: yes. Red: annual-only model.)
- Credit facility in place? Established during peak, not in trough? (Green: in place. Red: no facility or drawn reactively.)
- Seasonal trough months: when does cash hit minimum? What's the floor?

Domain ground truth (from `fls-hvac.md`): Operators who model annual cash look fine on paper and get surprised in April. 13-week model shows the trough 6–8 weeks before it arrives. Credit facility should be established during summer peak — banks respond to strength, not urgency.

## Pricing Dynamics (HVAC Market Context)

Domain ground truth (from `fls-hvac.md`):
- Maintenance contract pricing has been stable or declining in real terms for 5–7 years
- Most operators have not raised prices since 2019–2020
- Underlying cost structure (technician wages, equipment, fuel) has increased 25–35%
- Gap between market-rate and current pricing = operational upside on acquisition

Acquisition screen implication: an operator with below-market maintenance contract pricing is undervaluing its recurring revenue base. This is operational upside, not a red flag — IF the customer base is price-tolerant.

## Technician Retention (HVAC-Specific Intelligence)

Domain ground truth (from `fls-hvac.md`): Retention is the constraint, not recruitment. Companies that have solved recruitment but not retention are running faster on the treadmill.

Retention drivers confirmed from engagement data:
1. Trust that the shop backs technicians in customer disputes
2. Equipment investment (technicians working with quality tools feel valued)
3. Predictable scheduling (families value predictability over overtime premium)
4. Clear path to senior tech / crew lead

Screen questions:
- Average technician tenure — use as proxy for retention quality
- Why did the last 2 technicians leave? (Ask directly)
- Does the business have a defined career ladder? (SOPs, lead tech role)

## Equipment Age — Capital Exposure

| Asset | Consideration |
|-------|--------------|
| Service vans | Age + mileage determines near-term capex. >150K miles or >8 years = replacement likely Year 1–2 |
| HVAC test equipment | Manifold gauges, refrigerant recovery units — check calibration and age |
| Specialized tools | Duct testing equipment, combustion analyzers — verify against scope of work |

Request fleet log and last service records before LOI on any deal with fleet >5 vehicles.
