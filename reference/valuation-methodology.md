# Valuation Methodology: HVAC & Fire Safety Businesses

## Overview

Valuation in this space is driven by three overlapping methods:
1. **SDE/EBITDA Multiple** — primary method for operator-buyer deals
2. **Recurring Revenue (ARR/MRR) Layer** — separate valuation for service agreement base
3. **DSCR-Constrained Ceiling** — what SBA financing actually supports

In practice, the purchase price is bounded by what SBA will finance, not by what the seller wants.

---

## Method 1: SDE Multiple (Primary)

### What Is SDE?
Seller's Discretionary Earnings = net income + owner compensation + depreciation/amortization + one-time/non-recurring expenses + personal expenses run through the business.

SDE represents the total economic benefit to a working owner-operator. It is the standard earnings basis for small business acquisitions under $5M.

### SDE Add-Back Categories
**Add-backs that survive QoE scrutiny:**
- Owner's above-market salary (normalize to replacement cost: $80K–$120K for a working-owner HVAC operator)
- Owner's vehicle (personal use portion)
- Owner's health insurance (personal)
- One-time legal or professional fees
- Non-recurring equipment repairs

**Add-backs that require validation:**
- Owner's family members on payroll (what role do they actually fill?)
- "Discretionary" marketing or travel expenses
- Depreciation add-backs on aging fleet (will be capex shortly)

**Add-backs that are typically rejected:**
- Rent below market rate (owner-occupied real estate — normalize to market lease rate)
- Systematic underinvestment in fleet or equipment presented as an "add-back"

### Multiple Ranges by Business Type

**HVAC:**
| Profile | SDE Multiple Range |
|---------|-------------------|
| Best-in-class: recurring revenue >40%, 4+ techs, Sun Belt, clean concentration | 4.0–4.5x |
| Solid: recurring 25–40%, 2–3 lead techs, stable market | 3.0–4.0x |
| Average: recurring 15–25%, some owner-dep, serviceable team | 2.5–3.5x |
| Below average: install-heavy, owner-dependent, weak recurring | 1.5–2.5x |

**Fire & Life Safety:**
| Profile | EBITDA Multiple Range |
|---------|----------------------|
| Best-in-class: inspection-heavy, NICET-certified team, contracted accounts, >$400K EBITDA | 5.0–6.0x |
| Solid: inspection + install mix, some contracts, 2+ inspectors | 4.0–5.0x |
| Average: mixed services, owner-inspector, some commercial accounts | 3.0–4.0x |
| Below average: install-dominant or sub-$150K EBITDA with heavy owner-dep | 2.0–3.0x |

## Method 2: Recurring Revenue (ARR/MRR) Layer

Service agreement bases and monitoring contracts are valued separately from the EBITDA multiple. This is not additive to the purchase price in most operator-buyer deals — it justifies paying toward the top of the EBITDA multiple range. But in structured deals, it can be broken out explicitly.

### HVAC Maintenance Agreement Valuation
- Annual contract value × 2.0–3.0x = standalone ARR value
- Justification: sticky, low-churn, compliance-adjacent (HVAC warranties often require maintenance)

**Example:** 300 maintenance agreements × $250/yr avg = $75K ARR × 2.5x = $187K standalone value
→ This supports paying 0.5x higher on the EBITDA multiple than a comparable business without agreements

### F&LS Inspection Contract Valuation
- Annual inspection contract revenue × 2.5–3.5x = standalone value (higher multiple justified by compliance-mandated stickiness)
- Monitoring ARR (central station contracts) × 3.0–4.0x = standalone value (SaaS-like, very low churn)

**Example:** $400K inspection contract revenue × 3.0x = $1.2M standalone ARR value
→ In a deal where EBITDA multiple would be 4.0x, ARR layer supports 4.5–5.0x effective multiple

### When to Break Out the ARR Layer Explicitly
- When the seller is undervaluing their recurring revenue base
- When you want to offer a lower multiple on non-recurring revenue (installs, project work) while honoring the inspection/service base
- When structuring a deal to justify a higher price to an SBA lender

## Method 3: DSCR-Constrained Ceiling

The maximum price you can pay is bounded by what the SBA loan + seller note structure will support at a 1.25x DSCR minimum.

### Formula
**Maximum Annual Debt Service = SDE ÷ 1.25**
**Maximum Loan Amount** = back-calculated from max debt service at prevailing 10-year SBA rate

### Quick Ceiling Calculator

At 10.25% SBA rate, 10-year term:

| SDE | Max Debt Service (1.25x) | Max SBA Loan | Max Price (10% equity injection) |
|-----|--------------------------|--------------|----------------------------------|
| $200K | $160K | ~$1.19M | ~$1.32M |
| $300K | $240K | ~$1.79M | ~$1.99M |
| $400K | $320K | ~$2.38M | ~$2.65M |
| $500K | $400K | ~$2.98M | ~$3.31M |
| $600K | $480K | ~$3.57M | ~$3.97M |

> These are approximations. Use an amortization calculator with current WSJ Prime for exact figures.

**Key point:** If a seller is asking above the DSCR ceiling, the deal either requires a larger down payment, a larger seller note (reducing SBA loan), or a price reduction.

## Worked Example: Phoenix HVAC Company

**Business profile:**
- Revenue: $2.1M
- SDE: $380K
- Asking price: $1.4M (3.7x SDE)
- Service agreements: 340 (avg $240/yr = $81.6K ARR)
- 3 lead techs + 2 junior techs

**Step 1: SDE Multiple Check**
$380K × 3.7x = $1.406M ✓ — in the 3.5–4.0x range for a solid HVAC business

**Step 2: ARR Layer Sanity Check**
$81.6K ARR × 2.5x = $204K standalone value
→ Supports paying toward 4.0x; asking price is conservative

**Step 3: DSCR Ceiling Check**
- SDE: $380K → Max debt service: $304K
- SBA loan at 10.25%/10yr: ~$2.26M
- With 10% equity injection: supports up to ~$2.51M purchase price
- Asking price of $1.4M: well within SBA financing range ✓

**Step 4: Verdict**
Price is fair-to-favorable. The ARR layer justifies the multiple. DSCR supports a clean SBA deal. Proceed.

## Valuation Red Flags

- Seller pricing based on revenue multiple alone (e.g., "1x revenue") without reference to earnings
- EBITDA multiple feels high because seller has normalized owner salary to $0 (not standard)
- "Blue sky" premium claimed without documented customer relationships, brand, or IP
- Asking price derived from "what I need to retire" not from market multiples
- Install backlog presented as enterprise value (backlog is revenue, not a separate asset)
