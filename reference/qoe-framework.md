# QoE Framework: Pre-Quality of Earnings Analysis for Trades Businesses

## What a QoE Is (and Isn't)

A Quality of Earnings (QoE) report is a financial due diligence product produced by a third-party accounting firm. It validates the earnings claimed by the seller, normalizes add-backs, and identifies revenue quality issues.

**Cost:** $10,000–$25,000 for a small business QoE ($500K–$5M revenue range)
**When to engage:** After LOI, before final purchase price is locked

**What it does:**
- Validates that SDE/EBITDA is real and repeatable
- Tests revenue recognition (is revenue counted when earned or when invoiced?)
- Reviews add-backs and disallows unsupportable ones
- Identifies revenue concentration, seasonality normalization, and one-time items

**What it does NOT do:**
- Tell you if the business is a good deal
- Replace operational due diligence (truck inspections, tech interviews)
- Verify customer relationship durability

## Five Pre-QoE Tests

Run these before engaging a QoE firm. If a deal fails 2+, require seller to address issues or reduce price.

### Test 1: The Tax Return Reconciliation

**What to do:** Ask for 3 years of business tax returns (Form 1120-S, 1065, or Schedule C). Compare to the seller's P&L or CIM financials.

**What to look for:**
- Revenue on returns should match P&L within 5% (timing differences are normal; large gaps are not)
- Net income on returns is typically lower than SDE (add-backs explain the difference — ask for an itemized bridge)
- Large increases in COGS with no corresponding revenue increase = potential expense normalization issue

**Failing signal:** Seller refuses to provide tax returns, or the gap between CIM and returns exceeds 15% without a clear explanation.

---

### Test 2: The Recurring Revenue Test

**What to do:** Ask for the full service agreement / maintenance contract roster. For F&LS: request the inspection contract list with annual values and renewal dates.

**What to verify:**
- Actual count matches what was represented (±10% is normal attrition; ±25% is a red flag)
- Average contract value matches total ARR ÷ count
- Auto-renewal clauses present and current (not lapsed)
- No single contract represents >15% of total contract value

**Distortions to watch for:**
- "Active" agreements that haven't been invoiced in 12+ months (dormant ≠ active)
- Seasonal agreements presented as year-round recurring
- Annual contracts billed upfront: is revenue recognized over 12 months or in one lump? (Cash accounting inflation)

**Failing signal:** ARR cannot be verified from contract roster, or actual renewing ARR is <80% of represented ARR.

---

### Test 3: The Owner Compensation Normalization Test

**What to do:** Identify every line item of owner benefit in the financials. Build a normalized compensation bridge.

**Owner compensation components to find:**
- W-2 salary (owner + spouse/family members on payroll)
- Owner distributions (S-corp)
- Vehicle: personal use allocation
- Health insurance (owner's policy)
- Retirement contributions (SEP, SIMPLE, Solo 401k)
- Life insurance (personal benefit)
- Personal travel, meals, entertainment coded to business

**Normalize to:**
- Replacement cost for an owner doing the same operational role ($80K–$120K for working-owner HVAC; $100K–$140K for working-owner F&LS with inspector duties)
- Add back everything above replacement cost as part of SDE

**Distortion to watch for:**
- Owner pays themselves $40K and claims the rest as SDE — inflates the add-back
- Owner has family members at market rate on payroll — those are real costs, not add-backs
- Owner pays no salary at all: normalize DOWN by adding market rate salary before computing SDE

**Failing signal:** Owner compensation add-back represents >30% of total SDE claim without clear documentation.

---

### Test 4: The Revenue Quality Test

**What to do:** Request a revenue breakdown by type for the last 2–3 years.

**Categories to separate:**
1. Recurring service/inspection/maintenance revenue
2. Service call / repair revenue (reactive, variable)
3. New installation revenue (project-based)
4. Equipment sales (low-margin, should be separated)

**What to check:**
- Install revenue spike in the last 12 months (one large job inflating TTM EBITDA — test for repeatability)
- Year-over-year consistency in each category
- New construction or developer relationship revenue (cyclical, not recurring — discount to 0.5–1.0x in valuation)

**Seasonality normalization:** For HVAC, trailing twelve months (TTM) ending in October will look better than TTM ending in February. Always normalize to a full year.

**Failing signal:** Revenue quality breaks when annualized: TTM EBITDA is 30%+ higher than the 3-year average with no credible explanation.

---

### Test 5: The Working Capital and Balance Sheet Test

**What to do:** Review the last 2–3 years of balance sheets (or request from accountant). Focus on AR aging, inventory, and liabilities.

**Key items:**
- **Accounts Receivable aging:** >90-day AR exceeding 15% of total AR = collection problem or customer friction
- **Unbilled work:** Is there significant work completed but not yet invoiced? (Inflates apparent backlog)
- **Inventory:** HVAC equipment inventory at cost — is it current, or aging models that won't sell?
- **Deferred revenue:** Annual contracts collected upfront — seller is holding a liability that transfers to buyer
- **Personal guarantees:** Is the seller personally guaranteeing vendor lines? These may affect your ability to take over trade credit.

**For asset sales (most small business deals):** You are NOT buying the liabilities — but you need to know if there are payables, warranty obligations, or deferred revenue that will become your operational problem post-close.

**Failing signal:** AR aging shows >20% in 90+ days, or seller cannot provide a balance sheet at all.

## Trades-Specific Earnings Distortions

These are common manipulation points in HVAC and F&LS financials. Know them before you read any CIM.

### 1. Service Agreement Deferral Gaming
- Seller collects annual contracts in Q4 but the P&L shows revenue in Q4 (cash basis) rather than spreading over 12 months
- Impact: overstates Q4/full-year revenue; buyer inherits the obligation to service those contracts
- Fix: require accrual-basis restatement or adjust for deferred revenue liability

### 2. The "Bounce-Back" Year
- Business had a bad year (COVID, owner health issue, key tech departure), recovered in the TTM
- Seller uses TTM as the basis for SDE; 3-year average tells a different story
- Fix: always weight the 3-year average; ask what drove the recovery and whether it's structural

### 3. Fleet Underinvestment Add-Back
- Owner hasn't replaced trucks in 7 years; claims low depreciation as an add-back
- Reality: buyer will face $200K–$400K in fleet replacement in Years 1–3
- Fix: normalize fleet capex to replacement schedule; reduce SDE by normalized annual fleet cost

### 4. Cash-Basis Equipment Sales
- HVAC companies that do equipment-only sales (boilers, heat pumps) can front-load revenue in installation months
- May inflate December or March revenue if large equipment orders are billed at delivery
- Fix: recast on accrual basis; separate equipment revenue from service revenue

### 5. Related-Party Transactions
- Owner leases building to the business at below-market rate (artificially depresses expenses → inflates SDE)
- Owner uses family members in roles at non-market rates (either over or underpaid)
- Fix: normalize rent to market rate; replace family labor at market cost

### 6. Warranty Reserve Absence
- HVAC install businesses often carry no warranty reserve despite industry standard of 1–3% of install revenue
- Post-close warranty claims hit buyer with no reserve backing
- Fix: require warranty claims history for 3 years; build a reserve into working capital at close
