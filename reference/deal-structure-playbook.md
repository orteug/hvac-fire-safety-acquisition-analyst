# Deal Structure Playbook: HVAC & Fire Safety Acquisitions

## Structure Decision Tree

```
Is the deal above or below $5M purchase price?
├── Above $5M → SBA 7(a) max exceeded → conventional financing or SBA 504 only
└── Below $5M → SBA 7(a) eligible → proceed

Does the business qualify for SBA? (see eligibility checklist below)
├── No → conventional bank loan or seller-financed deal only
└── Yes → SBA 7(a) preferred structure

What is the DSCR headroom?
├── >1.5x → straightforward SBA deal; minimal seller note needed
├── 1.25–1.5x → tight but workable; seller note at standby may be needed
└── <1.25x → deal requires price reduction, larger down payment, or creative structure
```

## Asset Sale vs. Stock Sale

### Operator-Buyer Default: Asset Sale

In nearly all small business acquisitions under $5M, the buyer prefers an **asset sale**. The buyer purchases specific business assets — equipment, vehicles, customer contracts, goodwill — without assuming liabilities.

**Why buyers prefer asset sale:**
- No inherited liabilities (lawsuits, tax obligations, warranty claims, workers' comp history)
- Step-up in basis: buyer can depreciate assets at current market value, reducing tax burden
- SBA lenders strongly prefer asset sales

**Why sellers often prefer stock sale:**
- Capital gains treatment on the full purchase price (lower tax rate than ordinary income)
- Avoids triggering change-of-control clauses in customer contracts
- Simpler if there are complex vendor or lease agreements tied to the entity

**Negotiating the gap:**
- A seller demanding stock sale due to tax preference: offer a gross-up (buyer pays a slightly higher price to offset seller's tax difference)
- A seller demanding stock sale due to contract transferability: review actual contracts for assignment language — most commercial contracts allow assignment with reasonable notice

### When Stock Sales Make Sense
- F&LS businesses where AHJ approval is tied to the licensed entity (rare but verify)
- Businesses with vendor credit lines at favorable rates that would not survive an asset sale
- Deals where the real estate IS the entity's primary asset (less common in trades)

## SBA 7(a) Structure

### Eligibility Checklist
- [ ] U.S.-based for-profit business in an eligible NAICS code
- [ ] Buyer meets SBA "creditworthy" standard (credit score >680 preferred; no recent BK)
- [ ] Business not in an ineligible industry (gambling, speculation, financial services — N/A for trades)
- [ ] Purchase price ≤ $5M (SBA 7(a) max)
- [ ] No existing federal debt delinquency
- [ ] Seller cannot retain >49% ownership post-close

### Standard SBA 7(a) Structure for Trades Acquisition

```
Total Purchase Price: $X
├── SBA 7(a) Loan:     ~80–85% of purchase price
├── Buyer Equity:       ~10–15% of purchase price (cash down)
└── Seller Note:        0–10% of purchase price (on full standby)
```

**Minimum equity injection:** 10% of purchase price in cash (not borrowed)
**Standby requirement:** If seller note is included, it must be on full standby for 24 months (no payments to seller) per SOP 50 10 8

### Standard Terms (Q1 2026)
- Loan term: 10 years (business acquisition without real estate)
- Rate: Prime + 2.75% for loans >$50K, term >7 years (~10.25% at current Prime)
- Guarantee fee: 2.77% of guaranteed portion (on loans up to $700K); scales up for larger loans
- SBA guarantee: 75% for loans >$150K; 85% for loans ≤$150K

### SBA Lenders to Know
- **Preferred SBA Lenders (PLP status):** Can approve without SBA review — faster closing (30–60 days vs. 90+)
- **For trades acquisitions:** Live Oak Bank, Byline Bank, Newtek Business Finance, and many regional community banks with active SBA desks
- **Tip:** Interview 2–3 lenders before LOI. Pre-qualification letters are not commitments, but they establish credibility with sellers.

## Seller Note Structures

### Standard Seller Note
- Seller extends credit to buyer as part of the purchase price
- On full standby for 24 months per SBA rules (if SBA is involved)
- After standby period: monthly principal + interest payments
- Typical rate: 5–7% (negotiate; sellers often want market rate)
- Typical term: 3–7 years post-standby

**When to use:** Bridging a valuation gap; providing SBA with required equity; giving seller ongoing income post-close.

### Performance-Based Seller Note (SBA-Compliant Earnout)

SBA rules prohibit traditional earnouts (contingent payment based on post-close performance). The workaround: a **forgivable seller note** structured as a purchase price adjustment.

**Structure:**
- X% of purchase price is held as a seller note
- Forgiveness milestones tied to revenue, customer retention, or EBITDA performance over 12–24 months
- If milestones are met → seller note is forgiven (seller gets less; buyer keeps the forgiven amount)
- If milestones are not met → buyer pays the seller note on schedule

**Example:**
- Deal: $1.2M purchase price on $320K SDE
- Risk: 38% of revenue from 2 commercial accounts; owner is primary relationship
- Structure: $240K seller note (20% of purchase price) forgivable if revenue retention >85% at 12 months
- If revenue drops: buyer pays $240K to seller over 5 years (absorbs the loss with financed capital, not cash)
- If revenue holds: $240K stays with buyer — effective purchase price reduction

**SBA compliance note:** Structure must be approved by SBA lender. Document as a purchase price adjustment tied to specific, measurable conditions — not as "earnout." Get legal counsel to draft the note language.

### Consulting/Employment Agreement as Transition Structure
- Seller stays for 12–24 months as a paid consultant or part-time employee
- Compensation counted as a post-close expense, not part of purchase price
- Provides knowledge transfer, relationship continuity, and SBA-compliant seller engagement
- Typical: $5K–$10K/month for 12 months, declining in Year 2

**SBA note:** If seller compensation post-close is above market rate for their actual role, SBA may treat it as disguised additional purchase price. Keep comp proportionate to actual contribution.

## Escrow and Holdback Structures

### When to Use a Holdback
- Customer concentration risk (top 1–3 accounts represent >30% of revenue)
- Key employee risk (lead tech or inspector whose departure would affect revenue)
- Pending contract renewals at close
- Disclosed but unquantified liabilities (warranty claims, AHJ compliance issues)

### Standard Holdback Structure
- **Amount:** 10–20% of purchase price
- **Duration:** 12–18 months
- **Release condition:** Revenue retention above agreed threshold (typically 85–90% of trailing 12-month revenue)
- **Escrow agent:** Title company or third-party escrow agent holds funds; disburses on written confirmation of conditions

**Example:**
- $1.4M purchase price
- $140K (10%) held in escrow for 12 months
- Released if revenue stays above $1.78M (85% of $2.1M TTM)
- If revenue drops below threshold: holdback reduced pro-rata

## Letter of Intent (LOI) Essentials

An LOI is non-binding on price but creates a framework and typically includes a binding exclusivity clause.

**Must-haves in a strong LOI:**
- [ ] Purchase price and structure (all-cash, SBA, seller note breakdown)
- [ ] Asset sale vs. stock sale designation
- [ ] 30–45 day exclusivity period (binding)
- [ ] List of assets included/excluded
- [ ] Working capital target (to prevent seller from stripping the balance sheet pre-close)
- [ ] Conditions to closing (financing, due diligence, lease assignment)
- [ ] Target closing date
- [ ] Key employee retention language (if applicable)

**Exclusivity:** Do not issue an LOI without 30-day exclusivity minimum. If seller refuses exclusivity, walk away or assume you're in a competitive process — negotiate accordingly.

## Closing Timeline: SBA 7(a) Standard

| Phase | Duration | Key Actions |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Pre-LOI | 2–4 weeks | NDA, initial financials review, lender pre-qual |
| LOI to Diligence | 1 week | LOI signed, exclusivity starts, data room open |
| Diligence | 3–4 weeks | QoE, legal review, fleet/equipment inspection, tech interviews |
| SBA Underwriting | 3–6 weeks | Lender submits to SBA; PLP lenders faster |
| Closing Prep | 1–2 weeks | Lease assignment, contract novation, entity formation |
| **Total (typical)** | **8–14 weeks** | |

**Common closing delays:**
- Lease assignment requires landlord approval (start early; landlords are slow)
- SBA appraisal required if real estate involved
- SBA environmental review if fuel tanks, refrigerants, or hazmat on-site
- Customer contract assignment for F&LS businesses with formal assignment restriction clauses
